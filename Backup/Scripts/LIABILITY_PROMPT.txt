EVALUATION OF LIABILITY
Plaintiff's complaint specifically alleges two duplicative causes of action for
General Negligence and Premises liability. In order to succeed, Plaintiff must
demonstrate: (1) that ALR owned, leased, occupied or controlled the property; (2) that
ALR was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; (3) that Plaintiff was
harmed; and (4) that ALR's negligence was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff's
harm. Below is our analysis of these elements:
A. Ownership or Control
First, a Plaintiff must establish that the defendant owned, leased, occupied, or
controlled the property. Here, it is undisputed that ALR owned and operated the property
where the incident took place. As such, Plaintiff will be able to establish this element.
B. Negligent Use or Maintenance
Second, a plaintiff must establish that the defendant's use or maintenance of the
property was negligent. A defendant is negligent in the use or maintenance of the
property if (1) a condition of the property created an unreasonable risk of harm; (2) the
defendant knew or should have known about the condition; and (3) the defendant failed
to repair the condition, protect against harm from the condition, or give adequate warning
of the condition. (CACI No. 1003.)
Here, Plaintiff will allege that the property was negligently maintained because an
opening was left exposed on the scale platform. Plaintiff produced a photograph in
discovery that appears to show the opening covered by a metal cover plate.
Plaintiff will likely argue that the above photograph shows how the opening should have been covered at the time of the incident. Instead, the opening was left in an open position, creating a hazard for drivers exiting their vehicles:
We are in the process of investigating Plaintiff's allegations to determine whether
the opening is indeed typically covered as shown in the above photograph. According to
ALR, the opening had not been covered for at least 2 years prior to the incident. As such,
Plaintiff may have a viable argument that the condition as dangerous and ALR had notice
of the same for at least 2 years. However, even if Plaintiff is able to establish that a
dangerous condition existed, we will argue that Plaintiff was a regular user of the scale
house (Plaintiff had been at the scalehouse 41 times since September 2022) and that the
incident occurred during daylight hours. If an unsafe condition of the property is so
obvious that a person could reasonably be expected to observe it, then the one who
controls the property does not have to warn others about the dangerous condition. (CACI
1004.) We will argue that given Plaintiff's experience using the scale house and the lack
of visual obstructions, it should have been "obvious" to Plaintiff that there was an
opening in the platform and thus ALR had no duty to provide Plaintiff any additional
warnings. Even if these facts do not bar Plaintiff's claims, they will be useful to shift
some liability away from ALR due to Plaintiff's contributory negligence.
We will provide you with a further analysis of this element as more information is
obtained.
C. Harm
Third, a plaintiff must prove that they suffered damages from this incident. While
the extent of Plaintiff's injuries may be disputed, it does appear that Plaintiff was injured
as a result of the incident. As such, Plaintiff will be able to establish this element.
D. Substantial Factor
Last, a plaintiff must establish that the dangerous condition was a "substantial
factor" in the cause of the accident. CACI 430 defines substantial factors as follows:
"A substantial factor in causing harm is a factor that a reasonable person would
consider having contributed to the harm. It must be more than a remote or trivial
factor. It does not have to be the only cause of the harm."
Here, Plaintiff will argue that her injuries would not have occurred but for the
opening in the platform. Given the low bar for causation, Plaintiff will be able to
establish this element if he is able to show that a dangerous condition existed on the
property.
E. Summary
While it is premature to conclusively analyze liability, Plaintiff does have a viable
pathway to establish liability against ALR, as it appears that the joint was missing a metal
cover and had been in this condition for at least 2 years before the incident. On the other
hand, Plaintiff was an experienced user of the scalehouse and should have been aware of
the opening from prior visits. Moreover, the incident occurred during daylight house, and
there were no visual obstructions preventing Plaintiff from seeing the opening, such that
the opening should have been obvious to Plaintiff. We will also investigate whether
Plaintiff was wearing proper steel-toe shoes and corrective lenses at the time of the
incident as well as whether Plaintiff had a valid driver's license. Even if these facts do
not bar Plaintiff's claims, they will be useful to shift some liability away from ALR due
to Plaintiff's contributory negligence.
